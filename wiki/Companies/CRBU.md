## Part 0 — Investment Case

### Full Investment Case

**1. MISSION**
Develop next-generation CRISPR-edited allogeneic (off-the-shelf) cell therapies that solve the persistence and exhaustion problems of current CAR-Ts, starting with lymphomas and multiple myeloma.

**2. TEAM & DRIVE**
CEO Rachel Haurwitz, PhD (co-founder, UC Berkeley/Doudna lab, CEO since 2011). Scientific co-founder Jennifer Doudna, PhD (2020 Nobel Prize in Chemistry). Martin Jinek, PhD and James Berger, PhD as additional co-founders. Haurwitz has led Caribou from founding through IPO and all clinical milestones without a leadership change.

**3. PRODUCTS, SERVICES & GOALS**
Lead: CB-010 (chRDNA CRISPR-edited anti-CD19 CAR-T) — ANTLER Phase 1b trial ongoing; RMAT designation. CB-011 (anti-BCMA) for Multiple Myeloma — CaMMO Phase 1 ongoing; RMAT designation granted March 2026. CB-012 (anti-CD71) for AML. Revenue: Pipeline commercialization + platform (chRDNA) licensing.

**4. KEY METRICS IN 2–3 YEARS**
- CB-010 Pivotal Initiation: YE 2026 — path to BLA
- CB-011 Phase 1 Dose Expansion Data: H1 2026 — validates MM platform
- Cash: $320M (est. Q4 2025) — runway through 2026

**5. PRICE CATALYSTS**
- CB-011 RMAT data update (2026) — confirms dose expansion efficacy
- CB-010 pivotal trial design alignment with FDA (H2 2026)
- Potential partnership announcement for GALLOP (SLE) program

**6. ENTRY PRICE ANCHOR**
Current price ~$2.10. Market cap ~$186M. EV ≈ negative (cash exceeds cap). GREEN band: <$2.00 (Trading below net cash with two RMAT-designated clinical assets).

**7. THESIS KILLER**
Persistence data in the CB-010 pivotal trial fails to match autologous CAR-T durability (e.g., Yescarta), relegating allogeneic therapies to short-term bridging only.

**8. INVESTMENT HYPOTHESIS**
Caribou is trading for less than the cash on its balance sheet despite having two clinical-stage CRISPR therapies with FDA RMAT (Fast Track) status. The market has completely written off allogeneic cell therapy, but Caribou's chRDNA editing platform specifically targets the persistence problem that killed first-gen competitors.
